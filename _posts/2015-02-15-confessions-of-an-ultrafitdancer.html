Rachel Tipton

If you follow me on Facebook, you know how I love to spout off motivational stuff. I have always been that way, even back at Virginia School of the Arts. When I studied dance there in the early 90s, pre-Facebook, I would post daily inspirational quotes on my dorm room door. I am a silver-lining, never-give-up kind of survivor. Because of my past experiences and my daily struggle with Crohn’s disease, I believe that everyone can conquer whatever life dishes out, no matter how difficult. I try to help others see that strength in themselves through fitness and the #UltraFitLifestyle.

But, I have a dark side, just like anyone else.

My dark side comes out when I feel insecure or frustrated. I can be moody. I can be mean. I can get out of hand. I am passionate. As much energy I put out motivating others with all my might, I can put out in a negative way as well. Yikes, imagine that?!

I lost my confidence when I was pretty young and someone told me I was “fat.” I spiraled down the ugly path of eating disorders. I learned to drink to excess to dull the ache of insecurity and self-loathing. I allowed people to treat me with disrespect because I thought I deserved it.

But, when Crohn’s came along and I experienced a health smack down, I decided I deserve better. In fact, I deserve the best treatment from everyone, including myself! After quite a bit of soul searching, I took a long, hard look in the mirror and assessed the real me. The me that was trying to drown my pain in white wine and sweet treats. I knew I needed a change.

Quitting alcohol was a struggle, but it has been four years. I realized that drinking alcohol was having the same effect on my insides that it has when you pour it over an open wound. I could probably be just fine if I only drank the 5 oz of wine suggested as a serving. But, that is NOT how much wine I want to drink. I pretty much want to drink a whole bottle once I get started. Since it is all or nothing for me, I decided on nothing. At first, I felt deprived and cheated; it simply was not fair. But now, four years later, I observe the effects of alcohol on others and I don’t miss it at all.

I see people drink a bit too much and lose focus on their workouts. I observe that there is a link between how difficult it is for some to lose those pesky 10 lbs and how much or often they drink. Giving up alcohol is a choice that makes some people uncomfortable. Friends of mine know that my husband and I no longer drink, and we cannot help but wonder if that is why we are seldom invited to parties. Hmmm.

Does that hurt a little and send me to my dark side? Sure. However, I love this UltraFitLifestyle I have created for myself. I manage my Crohn’s without medication. I enjoy sharing my ways with others so they can sculpt their UltraFitLifestyle. There is no way I could run 1/2 marathons, train others, train myself, and keep the schedule I keep if I still drank alcohol. I would be in Crohn’s flare hell. I still struggle with the sugar addiction. Sometimes I simply cannot help myself as far as cupcakes are concerned.

I am laser focused on achieving the UltraFitLifestyle, but I remain a human with weaknesses.
